**To open a pool you need six things: chlorine shock (liquid or cal hypo), pH increaser and decreaser, alkalinity increaser (baking soda works), cyanuric acid stabilizer, algaecide as a backup, and fresh test supplies. Budget roughly $100 to $150 for a 15,000 gallon pool. Balance alkalinity and pH first, then shock, then stabilize.**

The exact list depends on what your water looks like after winter. A pool that closed clean under a tight cover might need $40 of chemicals. A green swamp needs triple the shock and a week of patience. Test first, buy second. Here is the full list, the sequence, and the numbers behind both.

That is the whole pool chemical opening kit. The boxed opening kits sold at pool stores bundle roughly these items for $50 to $90, and they are fine, but check the shock quantity. Most kits assume clear water and include only 1 to 2 lbs of shock, which will not touch a green pool.

Before any chemicals: the physical opening

Chemicals cannot fix what the filter and brush are for. Pull and clean the cover, top the water to mid skimmer, reinstall plugs and baskets, prime the pump, and get the filter running. Skim and brush everything. Run the pump for a few hours before you test, so the sample reflects the whole pool and not the dead layer on top.

The opening sequence, in order

Order matters because each chemical changes how the next one behaves. Chlorine added at pH 8.2 works at a fraction of its strength, and stabilizer added before shock slows the kill you are trying to get. Here is the sequence we run every spring.

1.  Test everything: free chlorine, pH, total alkalinity, CYA, and calcium hardness if you have a plaster pool or heater. Write the numbers down.

2.  Fix total alkalinity first, target 80 to 120 ppm. Baking soda raises it about 10 ppm per 1.5 lbs per 10,000 gallons. Alkalinity is the foundation; pH will not hold without it.

3.  Set pH to 7.2 to 7.8. Opening slightly low, around 7.2, makes the coming shock more effective. Add acid slowly with the pump running, in partial doses, retesting between.

4.  Shock the pool at dusk. Clear water: raise free chlorine to about 10 ppm. Green water: 20 to 30 ppm, which on 15,000 gallons means 3 to 4 gallons of 12.5% liquid chlorine or the cal hypo equivalent. Run the pump 24 hours.

5.  Hold chlorine elevated until the water clears, retesting and re dosing daily. Brush daily too; algae hides in a biofilm the brush breaks open.

6.  Once chlorine falls back below 5 ppm, test and adjust CYA to 30 to 50 ppm. If you will run trichlor tablets all summer, aim for the low end, because tablets add roughly 0.6 ppm of CYA per 1 ppm of chlorine and it accumulates all season. The full explanation is in our tablet guide at ClearCove Pools/pool-chlorine-tablets.

7.  Add a maintenance dose of polyquat algaecide, load the chlorinator or floater with tablets, and settle into the weekly routine. Swim once free chlorine is 1 to 4 ppm and pH is 7.2 to 7.8.

Those swim ready numbers are not ours alone. The CDC recommends 1 to 4 ppm free chlorine and pH 7.0 to 7.8 for residential pools; see https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html.

How much shock is enough?

The dose formula is the same one behind every pool calculation: gallons over 10,000, times the ppm you want to add, times 10.7 fl oz for 12.5% liquid chlorine. A 15,000 gallon pool going from 0 to 10 ppm needs 15,000 / 10,000 x 10 x 10.7 = about 160 fl oz, or 1.25 gallons of liquid chlorine. A green pool chasing 25 ppm needs about 3 gallons.

Chemical safety at opening

Opening is when people get hurt, because it is the one time a year you handle everything at once. Never mix chemicals, and especially never let trichlor tablets touch cal hypo shock; the combination can ignite or release toxic gas. Add each chemical to the water separately, never into the skimmer at the same time, and never add water to acid. Wear eye protection and gloves for acid and shock. Store everything sealed, dry, and separated. If last year's chemicals overwintered in a damp shed, caked shock and swollen containers go to household hazardous waste, not the pool.

FAQ

What chemicals do I need to open my pool for the first time?

Chlorine shock, pH increaser and decreaser, baking soda for alkalinity, cyanuric acid stabilizer, a quart of polyquat algaecide, and fresh test supplies. For a typical 15,000 gallon pool budget $100 to $150. Test the water before buying, because a clean spring opening may only need half the list.

Should I buy a pool opening kit or individual chemicals?

Kits are convenient and priced fairly at $50 to $90, but they assume clear water. If your pool wintered green, buy shock separately, since most kits include only 1 to 2 lbs and a green 15,000 gallon pool can need the equivalent of 3 gallons of liquid chlorine. Individual chemicals also let you skip what your test says you do not need.

How long after opening chemicals can you swim?

Wait until free chlorine falls to 1 to 4 ppm and pH sits between 7.2 and 7.8, verified by test, not by the calendar. After a standard 10 ppm shock that usually takes 24 to 48 hours of sun and circulation. After a heavy green cleanup it can take a week or more.

Do I need stabilizer at opening?

Test first. If CYA reads 30 to 50 ppm, you need none. Below 30, add cyanuric acid at about 1.3 oz per 1 ppm per 10,000 gallons, and add it after shocking, not before. If you run trichlor tablets, start at the low end of the range because tablets add CYA continuously all season.

Can I open a pool without shocking it?

Only if it closed late, opened early, and tests completely clean, which is rare. Winter water almost always carries algae spores and organic load a maintenance dose cannot burn through. Skipping shock at opening usually means fighting a bloom in June that costs more than the shock would have.

Bottom line

Opening chemicals are six items and one sequence: balance alkalinity, set pH, shock hard at dusk, clear the water, then stabilize and switch to tablets.
