# Chemicals needed to open a pool: the spring list and exact sequence

Opening a pool takes six chemical categories: alkalinity increaser, pH adjuster, calcium hardness increaser, stabilizer (cyanuric acid), sanitizer, and shock, added in that order with a test and a wait between each one. Add them out of order, especially shock before alkalinity, and you'll spend the first two weeks of the season chasing a reading that won't hold.

The order matters because alkalinity is the buffer that keeps pH stable. Adjust pH before alkalinity is in range and the pH drifts right back within a day, which is the single most common reason a spring opening turns into three trips to the pool store.

## What's the correct order for opening chemicals?

Test first. A full test (chlorine, pH, alkalinity, calcium hardness, cyanuric acid) tells you which of the six categories you actually need and how much, since a pool that overwintered with a cover on may need very little.

1. **Total alkalinity** to 80 to 120 ppm, using sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) to raise it or muriatic acid to lower it.
2. **pH** to the CDC-recommended 7.0 to 7.8 range, using soda ash to raise it or muriatic acid to lower it, only after alkalinity is dialed in.
3. **Calcium hardness** to 200 to 400 ppm using calcium chloride, since low hardness after a winter of dilution from rain and snowmelt lets the pool pull calcium off plaster and metal fittings instead.
4. **Cyanuric acid (stabilizer)** to 30 to 50 ppm for outdoor pools, so chlorine survives sunlight instead of burning off within hours.
5. **Sanitizer** (chlorine or bromine) brought up to a working residual, at least 1 ppm free chlorine per CDC guidance, or 2 ppm if the pool uses cyanuric acid.
6. **Shock** last, once everything else is dialed in, to clear out anything that grew during the off-season and to burn off combined chlorine from winter buildup.

Wait 15 to 30 minutes between each step with the pump running, and retest before moving to the next chemical. Skipping the wait means you're dosing off a reading the water hasn't caught up to yet.

## Why does alkalinity have to go first?

Total alkalinity is the water's resistance to pH change. A pool sitting at 40 ppm alkalinity will show wild pH swings after every chemical addition, every rain, and every top-off, because there's nothing buffering the water. Getting alkalinity into the 80 to 120 ppm range first means every chemical added afterward, especially pH adjusters, actually holds instead of bouncing back within hours.

For a typical 15,000-gallon pool sitting at 60 ppm alkalinity, reaching the low end of target (80 ppm, a 20 ppm rise) takes about 4.5 pounds of baking soda, based on the standard rate of 1.5 pounds per 10,000 gallons per 10 ppm of increase, added in a single dose since 20 ppm is under the per-treatment cap most manufacturers recommend.

## Why does cyanuric acid go before shock, not after?

Cyanuric acid takes 48 to 72 hours to fully dissolve, especially in cold spring water, so dosing it early gives it time to stabilize before the pool sees real UV exposure. It also determines your shock target: without any cyanuric acid protection, an outdoor pool can lose a full shock dose in 4 to 6 hours of direct sun, undoing the whole point of the treatment.

## When should you not shock right away?

If the water is still green or heavily clouded from an uncovered winter, skip straight to a heavy shock and hold off on stabilizer and calcium adjustments until the water clears and you can get an accurate test reading; adding stabilizer to green, cloudy water just wastes the product and delays an accurate CYA reading.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need all six chemicals every spring?**
No. A well-covered pool that was properly closed often only needs a shock and a chlorine top-off. A full six-step sequence is for pools that sat uncovered, had a leak, or show readings well outside target on the first test.

**How long after opening can I swim?**
Once free chlorine is in the CDC-recommended range (at least 1 ppm, or 2 ppm with cyanuric acid) and pH sits between 7.0 and 7.8, the water is safe. After a heavy shock, that's typically 24 to 48 hours, once the chlorine reading has come back down out of shock range.

**What order should I test in during opening week?**
Alkalinity and pH first, since they affect every other reading, then calcium hardness and cyanuric acid, then chlorine last, right before you decide on a shock dose.

**Can I add all six chemicals on the same day?**
Yes, but not all at once. Space each addition 15 to 30 minutes apart with the pump running and retest before the next one; doing this over a single afternoon is normal, doing it in one dump is how pools end up cloudy.

Bottom line: test first, then work alkalinity, pH, calcium, stabilizer, sanitizer, and shock in that order, with a wait and a retest between each. The sequence protects the money you're spending on each chemical.
