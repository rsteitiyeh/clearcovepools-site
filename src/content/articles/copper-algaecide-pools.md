Copper algaecide kills algae by disrupting the enzyme systems inside the algae cell, and it works faster than most alternatives against stubborn strains like mustard and black algae. The tradeoff is staining: copper that falls out of solution deposits as blue-green or teal discoloration on plaster, fiberglass, and even vinyl, and it happens fastest when pH climbs above the recommended range.

Whether copper algaecide is the right call for a specific pool depends less on the algae and more on the surface, the water chemistry discipline of the owner, and whether the pool has stained before.

## How does copper algaecide actually kill algae?

Copper ions bind to enzymes the algae cell needs for photosynthesis and respiration, starving the cell rather than oxidizing it the way chlorine does. That's why copper works even when chlorine demand is already maxed out fighting an active bloom; it attacks through a different mechanism entirely, so it stacks with a chlorine shock instead of competing with it.

Non-chelated copper products are the fastest-acting and cheapest but also the most prone to precipitating out of solution. Chelated copper compounds wrap the copper ion in a protective chemical shell that lets it dissolve slowly over weeks, using less total copper for the same kill while sharply cutting the staining risk.

## When does copper actually stain a pool?

Staining risk rises sharply once copper levels climb above roughly 0.3 ppm while pH also drifts upward; the combination is what pulls copper out of solution and onto the surface as a solid deposit. Keeping pH in the 7.2 to 7.6 band, the tighter operating range most pool professionals target inside the CDC's broader 7.0 to 7.8 guidance, is the single biggest thing an owner controls to prevent it ([CDC Healthy Swimming](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/about/home-pool-and-hot-tub-water-treatment-and-testing.html)).

High total alkalinity compounds the problem because it pushes pH up faster after every chemical addition, giving copper more opportunities to precipitate. A pool running alkalinity above 120 ppm alongside a copper algaecide is a much higher staining risk than the same dose in a properly balanced pool.

| Fiberglass | Moderate to high | Chelated copper, dosed conservatively |
| Vinyl liner | Moderate, worse if pH or alkalinity is out of range | Polyquat, or chelated copper only |

| Pool with light-colored or blonde-haired frequent swimmers | Not a surface risk, but copper can tint hair green | Polyquat |

## When should you skip copper algaecide entirely?

Skip it on a pool that has stained before; copper adds to a dissolved-metal load that's already prone to precipitating, and an ascorbic acid stain treatment won't fully work while more copper keeps entering the water. Skip it on white plaster or pebble pools where any staining is highly visible, unless you're using a chelated formula and can hold pH tightly for the treatment window. Skip it if pH and alkalinity are already running high and you don't have time to bring them down first, since dosing copper into unbalanced water is close to guaranteeing a stain.

Polyquat algaecide, at 60% concentration for chlorine-resistant strains, is the safer default anywhere staining is a real concern. It costs more per treatment and won't act quite as fast, but it carries no staining risk at any concentration.

## How do you use copper algaecide safely if you do choose it?

Balance pH to 7.2 to 7.6 and alkalinity to 80 to 100 ppm before adding it, not after. Use a chelated formula on any light-colored or previously stained surface. Dose at label rate, never double up to "get ahead" of an outbreak, since overdosing is what pushes copper past the precipitation threshold. Retest pH daily for the first week after treatment and correct any upward drift immediately.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does copper algaecide turn blonde hair green?**
It can, especially with repeated exposure and high pH, the same conditions that cause surface staining. Swimmers with light or chemically treated hair should rinse immediately after swimming in a copper-treated pool.

**How long does copper stay in the water after treatment?**
Non-chelated copper can precipitate out within days if pH rises. Chelated copper is designed to stay in solution for weeks, which is part of why it costs more per dose.

**Can I use copper algaecide and a copper-based ionizer at the same time?**
No. Running both risks pushing total copper well past the 0.3 ppm staining threshold without either system telling you it's happening; pick one copper source and test for it directly.

**Is polyquat as effective as copper against mustard algae?**
At 60% concentration, yes, polyquat is one of the two formulas (along with chelated copper) that actually holds up against mustard algae's chlorine-resistant coating. Lower-concentration polyquat sold for routine maintenance is not strong enough.

Bottom line: copper algaecide works fast and stacks well with a chlorine shock, but it stains once levels pass roughly 0.3 ppm and pH drifts up. Balance the water first, use a chelated formula on light surfaces, or skip copper for polyquat if the pool has stained before.
