**Answer:** Copper algaecide kills algae fast and cheap by poisoning algal cells with copper ions, and it works at very low doses. The catch is staining. Keep total copper under 0.2 to 0.3 ppm and hold pH in the 7.2 to 7.8 range, or copper will stain plaster, fittings, and blond hair.

That answer covers 90 percent of the decision. The rest is knowing when copper is the right tool, when polyquat 60 is worth the extra money, and how a metal sequestrant keeps copper dissolved instead of plated onto your pool walls. We work through all three below, with real numbers.

How copper actually kills algae

Copper ions pass through algal cell walls and shut down photosynthesis and enzyme activity inside the cell. The algae can't produce energy, so it dies and bleaches out. It's the same reason copper sulfate has been used in ponds and reservoirs for over a century.

Most pool copper algaecides are chelated copper, usually 3 to 7 percent copper by weight, bonded to an organic carrier that keeps the copper dissolved longer. Chelated products stain less than raw copper sulfate at the same dose. They still stain if you overdose them.

Copper is a killer, not just a preventive. That makes it a strong choice for an active green algae bloom, especially paired with shock. Chlorine does the heavy lifting; copper mops up cells the chlorine misses. Per the CDC, keep free chlorine at 1 to 4 ppm in a residential pool (https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html), and don't treat algaecide as a substitute for that residual.

Effectiveness vs staining: the copper trade-off

Copper's weakness is that it never leaves. Chlorine gets consumed and gases off. Copper stays in solution until it either gets diluted out by fresh water or falls out of solution onto your surfaces. Every dose you add stacks on top of the last one.

When copper falls out of solution, you get blue-green stains on plaster, gray-black stains when it oxidizes, green tint in blond and chemically treated hair, and scale on heater elements. High pH is the usual trigger. Above roughly 7.8, copper solubility drops sharply and it starts precipitating as copper carbonate.

Two numbers matter. First, keep measured copper under 0.2 ppm as your working ceiling, with 0.3 ppm as the level where staining risk gets real on plaster. Second, hold pH between 7.2 and 7.8, with total alkalinity at 80 to 120 ppm to keep pH from drifting. Test copper with a metals test kit before every re-dose, not on a schedule. If the test already shows 0.2 ppm, the pool doesn't need more copper no matter what the bottle's label says.

One operator note from our own testing: fill water matters more than most bottles admit. Our test fill ran 0.1 ppm copper straight from the tap (well water through copper plumbing). Half the label dose put us at the ceiling. Test your fill water before your first dose.

When to use polyquat 60 instead

Polyquat 60 is the main non-metal alternative, a 60 percent active polymer that binds to algal cell walls and disrupts them. No copper, so no metal staining, ever. It's also non-foaming, unlike the cheap linear quat algaecides that turn a pool into a bubble bath.

The trade-off is price and punch. Polyquat runs $25 to $40 per quart against $10 to $18 for a quart of 7 percent chelated copper (approximate 2026 prices), and it's better as a preventive than a killer. For an established black algae colony, copper plus aggressive brushing and shock usually wins.

Pair copper with a metal sequestrant

If you run copper at all, run a sequestrant with it. A sequestrant (usually phosphonic acid based) wraps around dissolved metal ions and keeps them from precipitating onto surfaces. It doesn't remove copper; it babysits it.

Dose sequestrant when you first add copper, then maintain monthly, because chlorine slowly breaks sequestrants down. If stains have already set, a sequestrant won't lift them. You'll want an ascorbic acid treatment or a targeted stain product, and we walk through that in ClearCove Pools/best-pool-stain-remover.

  - Test copper and iron before dosing anything.

  - Add sequestrant per label, wait 24 hours, then add copper algaecide if still needed.

  - Retest copper monthly and after every heavy fill-water top-up.

  - Keep pH at 7.2 to 7.8 and alkalinity at 80 to 120 ppm the whole time.

Dosing and water balance checklist

A typical 7 percent chelated copper label calls for roughly 8 to 12 fl oz per 10,000 gallons as an initial dose. Start at the low end. You can always add more; you can't easily take copper out. A $15 metals test kit is cheaper than an acid wash.

Chlorine and CYA still run the show. Hold free chlorine at 1 to 4 ppm, pH 7.2 to 7.8, alkalinity 80 to 120 ppm, and cyanuric acid at 30 to 50 ppm for a chlorine pool. Algae blooms in pools where CYA has crept over 100 and locked up the chlorine, and no algaecide fixes that. FAQ

Will copper algaecide turn my hair green?

It can. Copper above roughly 0.3 to 0.5 ppm binds to the proteins in hair, and blond, gray, or bleached hair shows it first. Chlorine gets blamed, but copper is the actual culprit. Keep copper under 0.2 ppm and rinse hair after swimming and it's a non-issue.

How much copper algaecide should I add per 10,000 gallons?

For a 7 percent chelated product, labels typically call for 8 to 12 fl oz per 10,000 gallons initially and 4 to 6 fl oz for maintenance. Test copper first. If your water already reads 0.2 ppm, skip the dose entirely. Fill water and previous doses accumulate.

Can I swim right after adding copper algaecide?

Most labels allow swimming after about 15 to 30 minutes once the product has circulated, but read your specific label. The bigger gate is chlorine: confirm free chlorine is back in the 1 to 4 ppm range per CDC guidance before anyone swims, especially after shocking.

Is polyquat 60 worth the extra cost over copper?

For weekly prevention on a plaster pool, yes. Zero staining risk means zero sequestrant cost and zero stain-removal projects, which usually eats the price difference. For killing an active bloom on a budget, copper still earns its keep if you test and cap it at 0.2 ppm.

Does copper algaecide replace chlorine?

No. Copper kills algae but it's slow against bacteria and does nothing for swimmer waste. You still need a 1 to 4 ppm free chlorine residual at all times. Treat algaecide as a supplement to a properly chlorinated pool, never a substitute.

Bottom line

Copper algaecide is the cheapest effective algae killer you can buy, and it's safe for your surfaces if you respect one number: 0.2 ppm measured copper, hard ceiling 0.3. Test before every dose, hold pH at 7.2 to 7.8, and run a sequestrant alongside it. If you'd rather never think about metal staining, pay up for polyquat 60 and spend your attention on chlorine instead.
