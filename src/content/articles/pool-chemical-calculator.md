**A pool chemical calculator turns three inputs, your pool volume in gallons, your current test reading, and your target level, into an exact dose in ounces or pounds. The core formula: dose equals gallons divided by 10,000, times the ppm change you want, times a factor for each chemical. The free ClearCove Pools calculator runs this math for chlorine, pH, alkalinity, and stabilizer.**

You can absolutely do this math by hand, and we think every pool owner should do it once. After that, let the tool do it. This guide walks through the full dosing math with worked examples so the calculator is never a black box.

Step one: know your pool volume

Every dose scales with gallons, so a wrong volume makes every future dose wrong by the same percentage. Measure your pool, do not trust the number the previous owner remembered.

Volume formulas by shape

|                   |                                                    |                                  |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Pool shape**    | **Formula (feet in, gallons out)**                 | **Example**                      |
| Rectangle         | Length x Width x Avg Depth x 7.5                   | 32 x 16 x 5 x 7.5 = 19,200 gal   |
| Round             | Diameter x Diameter x Avg Depth x 5.9              | 24 x 24 x 4 x 5.9 = 13,594 gal   |
| Oval              | Length x Width x Avg Depth x 6.7                   | 30 x 15 x 4.5 x 6.7 = 13,568 gal |
| Kidney / freeform | Longest x Widest x Avg Depth x 6.0 (approximation) | 28 x 14 x 5 x 6.0 = 11,760 gal   |

Average depth is shallow end plus deep end, divided by 2. A pool that runs 3 ft to 7 ft has an average depth of 5 ft. The 7.5 factor is just gallons per cubic foot of water (one cubic foot holds 7.48 gallons; we round to 7.5).

Step two: know your targets

Dosing math needs a destination. These are the accepted residential targets. The CDC recommends 1 to 4 ppm free chlorine and pH 7.0 to 7.8 for residential pools; their swimming water guidance lives at https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html.

![](/media/5a59dddafa3d7bd4705ecd1be8c7765724361aa7.png)

  - Free chlorine: 1 to 4 ppm.

  - pH: 7.0 to 7.8, with 7.4 to 7.6 the sweet spot.

  - Total alkalinity: 80 to 120 ppm.

  - Cyanuric acid (stabilizer): 30 to 50 ppm for chlorine pools.

The gap between your test reading and the target is the ppm change you are dosing for. Test first, always. Dosing without a fresh reading is guessing with extra steps.

Step three: the dose formula

Nearly every pool dose reduces to one pattern:

**Dose = (gallons / 10,000) x ppm change x chemical factor**

The chemical factor is how much of a given product moves 10,000 gallons by 1 ppm. Those factors come from molecular weight and the product's strength, and they are the numbers baked into any honest pool chlorine dosage calculator.

Common chemical factors (per 1 ppm in 10,000 gallons)

|                     |                             |                                                                            |
| ------------------- | --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Goal**            | **Chemical**                | **Amount per 1 ppm per 10,000 gal**                                        |
| Raise free chlorine | Liquid chlorine, 12.5%      | 10.7 fl oz                                                                 |
| Raise free chlorine | Cal hypo, 65%               | 2.0 oz by weight                                                           |
| Raise free chlorine | Trichlor tablet, 90%        | 1.5 oz by weight                                                           |
| Raise alkalinity    | Baking soda (sodium bicarb) | 2.4 oz by weight (per 1 ppm TA)                                            |
| Raise CYA           | Cyanuric acid               | 1.3 oz by weight                                                           |
| Lower pH            | Muriatic acid, 31.45%       | Depends on TA; roughly 12 fl oz drops pH about 0.1 in 10,000 gal at TA 100 |

pH is the one exception to the tidy pattern. Acid demand depends on alkalinity, because alkalinity is literally the water's resistance to pH change. That is why good calculators ask for your TA before recommending an acid dose.

Worked example 1: raising chlorine

Pool: 15,000 gallons. Test says free chlorine is 0.5 ppm. Target: 2 ppm. The change needed is 1.5 ppm.

1.  Scale for volume: 15,000 / 10,000 = 1.5.

2.  Multiply by ppm change: 1.5 x 1.5 = 2.25.

3.  Apply the factor for 12.5% liquid chlorine: 2.25 x 10.7 = 24 fl oz.

So about three quarters of a quart of liquid chlorinating solution brings this pool from 0.5 to 2 ppm. Notice how forgiving that is. Even if your volume estimate is off by 10 percent, you land within a quarter ppm of target.

Worked example 2: raising alkalinity

Same 15,000 gallon pool. Alkalinity tests at 60 ppm, target 90 ppm, so we need a 30 ppm lift.

4.  Volume scale: 15,000 / 10,000 = 1.5.

5.  Times ppm change: 1.5 x 30 = 45.

6.  Times the baking soda factor: 45 x 2.4 = 108 oz, which is 6.75 lbs.

Just under 7 lbs of plain baking soda. The grocery store kind is chemically identical to the pool store's alkalinity increaser, usually at half the price. Add it in thirds over a day with the pump running, retesting between additions, because bicarb also nudges pH up slightly.

Worked example 3: lowering pH safely

Our 15,000 gallon pool reads pH 8.0 with TA at 100 ppm. Target pH 7.5. At that alkalinity, roughly 12 fl oz of 31.45% muriatic acid per 10,000 gallons moves pH about 0.1, so a 0.5 drop wants about 1.5 x 5 x 12 = 90 fl oz total. We would never add that at once.

Acid safety is not optional. Add acid to water, never water to acid. Pour slowly into the deep end return stream with the pump running, wearing eye protection and gloves, standing upwind. Dose no more than a third of the calculated amount, wait 4 hours with the pump on, retest, repeat. Acid overshoot corrodes heaters and stings swimmers, and walking pH back up wastes chemicals.

Worked example 4: shock dosing

Shocking uses the same formula with a bigger ppm target. Say our 15,000 gallon pool turned light green over a rainy week. Free chlorine reads 0, CYA reads 40. For a proper kill we want to reach roughly 40 percent of CYA, so about 16 ppm.

7.  Volume scale: 15,000 / 10,000 = 1.5.

8.  Times ppm change: 1.5 x 16 = 24.

9.  Times the liquid chlorine factor: 24 x 10.7 = 257 fl oz, almost exactly 2 gallons of 12.5% liquid chlorine.

Add it at dusk with the pump running, brush the walls, and retest in the morning. If the water consumed most of that chlorine overnight, the algae is still eating it; dose again. Two gallons sounds like a lot until you run the math, which is exactly why eyeballing shock doses fails so often.

Unit conversions the labels assume you know

Product labels flip between fluid ounces, dry ounces, pounds, and gallons, and mixing them up is the most common dosing error we hear about. Keep these straight:

|                       |                                                                       |
| --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Conversion**        | **Value**                                                             |
| 1 gallon              | 128 fl oz                                                             |
| 1 quart               | 32 fl oz                                                              |
| 1 lb (dry)            | 16 oz by weight                                                       |
| 1 cubic foot of water | 7.48 gallons                                                          |
| 1 ppm                 | 1 part per million, about 0.13 oz of pure substance per 1,000 gallons |

Note that fluid ounces measure volume and dry ounces measure weight. A cup of granular cal hypo does not weigh 8 oz. For dry chemicals, a cheap kitchen scale dedicated to pool duty ($12 or so) removes the guesswork, and it is one of the best small purchases we have made for pool care.

Calcium hardness and the numbers beyond dosing

Chlorine, pH, alkalinity, and CYA cover weekly care, but plaster pools and heaters add one more number: calcium hardness, with a working target of 200 to 400 ppm. Low calcium water pulls calcium out of plaster and grout; high calcium scales heater elements and clouds water. Raising it is easy with calcium chloride at about 1.25 oz per 1 ppm per 10,000 gallons. Lowering it, like CYA, only happens through dilution.

Serious water balancers combine pH, alkalinity, calcium, and temperature into a saturation index that predicts whether water wants to scale or corrode. You do not need to compute it by hand; you do need to know it exists, because it explains why two pools with identical pH can treat their equipment very differently.

Reading your test kit like the calculator does

A calculator is only as good as the numbers you feed it. Strips are fine for daily free chlorine and pH checks. For alkalinity, CYA, and anything you are about to dose acid against, a drop kit (Taylor K-2006 or similar, about $80 to $110 in 2026) reads meaningfully tighter. We test strips daily in season, drops weekly, and always drops before an acid dose.

Sample from elbow depth away from returns. Surface water near a return jet reads whatever you dosed an hour ago, not what the pool actually holds.

Product strength: the input everyone forgets

Every chlorine factor above assumes the strength printed on the label, and real products drift from it. Liquid chlorine is the worst offender. A jug labeled 12.5% can test under 8% after a month in a 90 degree garage, because sodium hypochlorite loses strength with heat and time. If your dose keeps coming up short, the math is fine and the jug is stale.

Buying rules we follow: buy liquid chlorine fresh, from a store that turns over inventory, in quantities you will use within 2 to 3 weeks in summer. Check date codes when they exist. Store jugs cool and shaded, never in a hot shed. Dry chemicals are far more stable: trichlor tablets and cal hypo hold strength for years if kept sealed and dry.

Strength also changes the dose linearly, so the correction is easy. If you only have 10% liquid instead of 12.5%, multiply the calculated dose by 12.5 / 10, or 1.25. The ClearCove Pools calculator has a strength field for exactly this reason, and it is worth the extra five seconds every time.

How often to test and dose

The math only works on a schedule. In swim season we test free chlorine and pH twice a week and after every party or storm, alkalinity weekly, and CYA monthly. Off season, once every week or two is plenty. Each test takes two minutes, and it is the difference between $10 of routine adjustment and $80 of algae recovery.

Dose one chemical at a time, in this order when several need fixing: alkalinity first, then pH, then chlorine, then stabilizer. Alkalinity is the buffer everything else sits on, so setting it first keeps the later doses from moving targets. Wait at least 4 hours of pump circulation between different chemicals, and never pre mix anything in the same bucket.

When the math says something weird

If the calculator asks for a dose that seems huge, believe your skepticism and retest before dosing. The usual culprits: a stale volume number, a strip read in bad light, or CYA so high that free chlorine targets shift. FAQ

How much chlorine do I add per 10,000 gallons?

To raise free chlorine 1 ppm in 10,000 gallons: about 10.7 fl oz of 12.5% liquid chlorine, 2 oz of 65% cal hypo, or 1.5 oz of trichlor. Multiply by your ppm gap and your volume in ten thousands. A pool at 0 ppm heading to 3 ppm in 20,000 gallons needs about 64 fl oz, half a gallon, of 12.5% liquid.

How do I calculate my pool volume in gallons?

Rectangles: length x width x average depth x 7.5. Round pools: diameter squared x average depth x 5.9. Average depth is the shallow plus deep ends divided by 2. Measure in feet, and remember the factor is just gallons per cubic foot. When in doubt, the ClearCove Pools/calculator volume tab handles odd shapes.

Is a pool chemical calculator accurate?

The math itself is exact chemistry. Real world accuracy depends on your volume estimate and test quality, which is why we recommend a drop kit for alkalinity and CYA. Expect results within about 10 percent, which is well inside the safe target ranges of 1 to 4 ppm chlorine and 80 to 120 ppm alkalinity.

How much baking soda does it take to raise alkalinity 10 ppm?

About 1.5 lbs per 10,000 gallons. The factor is 2.4 oz per 1 ppm per 10,000 gallons, so 10 ppm x 2.4 oz = 24 oz. Scale for your volume: a 15,000 gallon pool needs about 2.25 lbs. Add in stages with the pump running and retest after a few hours.

Bottom line

All pool dosing is one formula: gallons over ten thousand, times the ppm change, times the chemical's factor. Measure your volume once, test before every dose, and split acid additions into thirds.
