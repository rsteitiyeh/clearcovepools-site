A 50 lb bucket of calcium hypochlorite pool shock costs $200 to $270 as of mid-2026, depending on the available-chlorine strength (65% to 70%), and it covers roughly 20 to 40 standard shock treatments for a 20,000-gallon pool. That works out to $6 to $11 per treatment, against $9 to $15 for a single 1 lb bag bought individually, so the bucket wins on a per-treatment basis once you'll use more than about six doses in a season.

The exact math depends on two things most buyers skip past: the product's actual available-chlorine percentage (not every "cal-hypo" is the same strength) and how many gallons your pool holds. Get both numbers right and the bucket-versus-bag decision becomes arithmetic, not guesswork.

## How much actual chlorine is in a 50 lb bucket?

Calcium hypochlorite (cal-hypo) products sold in 50 lb buckets carry 65% to 73% calcium hypochlorite by weight, which translates to 65% to 70% "available chlorine," the figure that actually determines dosing power. A standard 68% cal-hypo bucket, like In The Swim's 50 lb Calcium Hypochlorite Pool Shock, lists 65% minimum available chlorine ([In The Swim product page](https://www.intheswim.com/p/calcium-hypochlorite-pool-shock-bucket---50-lbs./400601.html)). A stronger formulation, like Leslie's Power Powder Pro, uses 73% calcium hypochlorite for 70% minimum available chlorine ([Leslie's product page](https://lesliespool.com/leslies-power-powder-pro-calcium-hypochlorite-pool-shock-50-lbs./18808.html)). That 5-point spread in available chlorine means the stronger bucket delivers about 8% more sanitizing power pound for pound, so a straight price-per-pound comparison between two "50 lb buckets" understates the difference unless you check the label percentage first.

## How many treatments does a 50 lb bucket actually cover?

Here's the math worked two ways, using each manufacturer's own label directions rather than a generic chart.

**68% product (In The Swim, $199.99 as of July 2026):** the label directs 1 lb per 10,000 gallons for a weekly shock, delivering 5 to 10 ppm available chlorine ([In The Swim product page](https://www.intheswim.com/p/calcium-hypochlorite-pool-shock-bucket---50-lbs./400601.html)). A 20,000-gallon pool takes 2 lb per treatment, so a 50 lb bucket delivers 25 treatments. At $199.99 for the bucket, that's $8.00 per treatment.

**73% product (Leslie's Power Powder Pro, $269.99 as of July 2026):** the label directs 5 to 7 oz per 10,000 gallons for routine unstabilized dosing ([Leslie's product page](https://lesliespool.com/leslies-power-powder-pro-calcium-hypochlorite-pool-shock-50-lbs./18808.html)). Scaled up to a full shock dose for a 20,000-gallon pool (roughly 12 oz per 10,000 gallons, doubled for a shock-strength treatment), that's about 24 oz, or 1.5 lb per treatment. A 50 lb (800 oz) bucket at that rate covers about 33 treatments, working out to $8.18 per treatment.

Both land in the same $8-per-treatment neighborhood despite the price gap, because the stronger product needs less material per dose. That's the actual apples-to-apples comparison, not the sticker price on the bucket.

## What's the correct dose for a green pool, not just routine shock?

A green or algae-heavy pool needs breakpoint chlorination, not a routine weekly dose. The standard industry formula targets free chlorine at roughly 10 times the combined chlorine (chloramine) reading, and severe algae or organic loads can push that multiplier to 20 or 30 times ([In The Swim breakpoint chlorination guide](https://www.intheswim.com/eguides/breakpoint-chlorination.html)).

Worked example: a 20,000-gallon pool tests at 2 ppm combined chlorine, so the breakpoint target is a 20 ppm free-chlorine rise. Using the 68% product's label rate (1 lb per 10,000 gallons delivers roughly 7.5 ppm, the midpoint of its 5 to 10 ppm range), reaching a 20 ppm rise across 20,000 gallons takes about 5.3 lb, roughly 11% of a single 50 lb bucket in one treatment. That's why a green-pool cleanup can burn through a fifth of a bucket in a single dose, well beyond what a routine weekly shock uses.

## How long does a 50 lb bucket keep, and how should it be stored?

Cal-hypo is a Class 3 oxidizer under NFPA classification and will self-decompose if stored above 125°F for extended periods, generating heat and potentially hazardous gases ([Safety Data Sheet, Accu-Tab calcium hypochlorite](https://labelsds.com/images/user_uploads/Accutab%20SDS%203-9-23.pdf)). Store the bucket in a cool, dry, well-ventilated spot out of direct sun, keep the lid tight between uses, and never store it near trichlor or dichlor tablets, liquid chlorine, acids, or anything combustible; cross-contamination between cal-hypo and other pool chemicals is a documented fire and explosion risk, not a theoretical one. Use a dedicated dry scoop for the bucket and never introduce a wet scoop, which speeds decomposition inside the container.

Sealed and stored correctly, a 50 lb bucket holds most of its strength for about a year; available chlorine degrades gradually even under good conditions, so buying two seasons' worth at once to chase a bulk discount usually costs more in lost potency than it saves in price.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is a 100 lb bucket cheaper per treatment than a 50 lb bucket?**
Usually, by a modest margin, since bulk pricing improves with volume. But a 100 lb bucket only pays off if you'll use it within a season; unused cal-hypo loses potency in storage, so buy to your actual season's dosing needs rather than the biggest container on the shelf.

**Can I mix cal-hypo with liquid chlorine or chlorine tablets in the same feeder or storage bin?**
No. Never mix calcium hypochlorite with trichlor, dichlor, liquid chlorine, or any other pool chemical; manufacturer safety data sheets warn this combination can trigger a violent exothermic reaction. Store and dose them separately, always.

**Why does cal-hypo raise calcium hardness over time?**
Because calcium is roughly a third of the compound by weight; every dose adds some calcium along with the chlorine. Pools shocked with cal-hypo weekly for a full season should have calcium hardness tested periodically, since levels above 400 ppm start contributing to scale on plaster and equipment.

**Is pool shock the same thing as cal-hypo?**
Not always. "Pool shock" is a category that includes calcium hypochlorite, dichlor, and non-chlorine (potassium monopersulfate) options; cal-hypo is simply the most common bulk-bucket format because it's the least expensive per pound of available chlorine among chlorine-based shocks.

Bottom line: check the available-chlorine percentage on the label before comparing prices, expect $6 to $11 per treatment from a 50 lb bucket against $9 to $15 per single bag, and never store cal-hypo near other pool chemicals.
