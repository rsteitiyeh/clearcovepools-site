**Answer:** A 50 lb bucket of cal-hypo pool shock (65 or 73 percent available chlorine) is the cheapest solid shock per pound of actual chlorine delivered, typically $170 to $240 per bucket. Dichlor costs more per unit of chlorine and adds cyanuric acid. Liquid chlorine often beats both if you can haul it.

Buying bulk pool shock is a math problem wearing a marketing costume. Labels sell you pounds of product; your pool only cares about pounds of free chlorine (FC). Below we compare cal-hypo, dichlor, and liquid on the number that matters, then cover the storage rules that keep a 50 lb oxidizer bucket from becoming a garage fire.

The three bulk shock chemistries

Cal-hypo: the bulk workhorse

Calcium hypochlorite comes in 65 percent and 73 percent available chlorine grades. It's unstabilized, so it doesn't add cyanuric acid, which makes it the right shock for pools already running trichlor tablets. It does add calcium: roughly 0.7 to 0.8 ppm calcium hardness per 1 ppm of FC added. On a soft-water fill that's harmless for years; on already-hard water it eventually pushes you toward scale.

Dichlor: stabilized, and that's the problem in bulk

Sodium dichlor runs about 55 percent available chlorine and adds roughly 0.9 ppm of cyanuric acid for every 1 ppm of FC. Shock a 15,000 gallon pool by 10 ppm with dichlor and you've added about 9 ppm CYA in one afternoon. Do that a few times and you blow past the 30 to 50 ppm CYA range a chlorine pool should hold, and your chlorine gets progressively lazier.

Dichlor in a 50 lb bucket only makes sense for fresh fills and low-CYA pools, and even then it's the priciest option per pound of FC. For comparison, trichlor tablets are about 90 percent available chlorine and add roughly 0.6 ppm CYA per 1 ppm FC, which is why tabs plus cal-hypo shock is the classic pairing. Liquid chlorine: no bucket, no residue

Pool-grade sodium hypochlorite is 10 to 12.5 percent available chlorine. It adds nothing but a little salt and a small pH rise. The downsides are weight (a full shock dose is several gallons), a short shelf life (it loses noticeable strength within weeks in a hot garage), and no 50 lb bucket convenience. Where a local supplier does carboy refills cheap, it's often the best deal running.

Storage safety: a 50 lb bucket is a serious oxidizer

This section matters more than the price math. Cal-hypo is a strong oxidizer. It doesn't burn by itself, but it makes everything near it burn hotter and faster, and it can self-heat to ignition if contaminated. Warehouse fires have started from a single contaminated drum.

**Never mix cal-hypo with trichlor.** Not in a feeder, not in a scoop, not by pouring one into a bucket that held the other. The two react violently and can produce heat, chlorine gas, and fire. One scoop per chemical, labeled, forever. This is the single most common serious pool-chemical accident, and it's fully preventable.

  - Store the bucket cool, dry, and off the concrete floor, away from direct sun. Heat and moisture are what set cal-hypo off.

  - Keep it away from acids, trichlor, fertilizer, gasoline, paint thinner, and brake fluid. A garage shelf over the lawnmower is the wrong spot.

  - Keep the lid sealed. Humid air degrades the product, and caked cal-hypo doses unpredictably.

  - Wear chemical splash goggles and nitrile gloves when scooping; the dust is rough on eyes and lungs.

  - Pre-dissolve or broadcast per label, always adding chemical to water, never water into the bucket.

  - If a bucket gets wet inside or starts smelling strongly of chlorine, move it outdoors away from structures and call your local hazmat non-emergency line. Don't reseal it and hope.

The CDC's healthy swimming page (https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html) is also the reference for when the water is safe again: get free chlorine back down into the 1 to 4 ppm residential range, with pH 7.0 to 7.8, before anyone swims after a shock.

Which bulk shock should you buy?

Our decision tree, short version. If you run trichlor tabs in season, buy 65 percent cal-hypo in the 50 lb bucket and watch calcium hardness once a month. If your fill water is already over about 300 ppm calcium, lean on liquid chlorine instead and accept the hauling. Only buy bulk dichlor for a fresh fill that needs CYA anyway, and stop using it once CYA hits 30 to 50 ppm.

Keep the rest of the water honest while you shock: alkalinity 80 to 120 ppm so pH doesn't bounce, CYA 30 to 50 ppm so your chlorine stays active in sunlight. A shock dose fixes a symptom; balanced water prevents the next one. FAQ

How long does a 50 lb bucket of pool shock last?

Sealed and stored cool and dry, cal-hypo keeps most of its strength for 2 to 3 years, losing a few percent of available chlorine per year. For a 15,000 gallon pool it's roughly 26 full 10 ppm shock treatments, so the product easily outlasts one season. Buy the bucket.

Is bulk pool shock cheaper than 1 lb bags?

Almost always. One-pound bags of 65 percent cal-hypo commonly run $6 to $8 each, while a 50 lb bucket lands near $3.60 per pound of product (both approximate). That's roughly 45 percent savings on the same chemical, as long as you can store it safely.

Can I use cal-hypo shock in a pool with a trichlor feeder?

Yes, and it's the standard pairing, but the two chemicals must never touch each other outside the pool. Broadcast or pre-dissolve cal-hypo directly into the pool, never into the skimmer, and never share scoops or buckets between the two products.

Does bulk pool shock raise pH?

Cal-hypo raises pH slightly and adds calcium; liquid chlorine nudges pH up a little; dichlor is close to neutral. In practice, retest pH the day after any big shock and bring it back into 7.2 to 7.8 with acid if it drifted. Alkalinity at 80 to 120 ppm keeps the drift small.

What's the cheapest way to shock a pool?

Per pound of actual chlorine, liquid chlorine at local refill prices (around $4.80 per lb FC at $5 a gallon) and 65 percent cal-hypo in 50 lb buckets (around $5.54) usually finish within pennies of each other. Whichever is cheaper and easier to store safely in your area wins.

Bottom line

Buy bulk shock on cost per pound of free chlorine, not price per bucket. By that measure, 65 percent cal-hypo in the 50 lb size and locally refilled liquid chlorine are the two winners, dichlor is the expensive way to inherit a CYA problem, and storage discipline is non-negotiable: dry, cool, sealed, and never anywhere near trichlor or acid.
