**The best pool stain remover depends on the stain. Metal stains (iron, copper) lift with ascorbic acid and stay away with a sequestrant. Organic stains (leaves, algae) respond to chlorine shock and enzymes. Identify first with a vitamin C tablet test, then treat. Guessing wastes money.**

Identify the stain before you buy anything

Stain removers are cheap compared to the chemicals people waste using the wrong one. Ascorbic acid does nothing for a leaf stain, and shocking a copper stain can actually set it darker. Ten minutes of identification saves the whole project.

Color gives you the first clue. Green-brown blotches under trees usually mean organic. Rust-colored spots, especially near rails, screws, or heater returns, point to iron. Blue-green or gray-black stains often mean copper, frequently from an algaecide or a corroding heat exchanger.

The vitamin C tablet test (for metal)

Hold a plain vitamin C tablet against the stain for 30 to 60 seconds, or crush a few tablets in a sock and hold that on it. If the spot lightens or vanishes, you have a metal stain, almost certainly iron. This is the single most useful diagnostic in pool stain work and it costs about a dollar.

The chlorine test (for organic)

Put a trichlor tablet or a small pile of granular chlorine in a thin sock and rest it on the stain for a few minutes (on a pole for deep spots). If the stain fades, it is organic. If neither test moves it, you may be looking at scale deposits or a dye issue rather than a true stain.

Treating metal stains: ascorbic acid and sequestrants

Ascorbic acid (vitamin C in bulk powder form) is the workhorse for iron stains and helps on copper. The standard dose is about 0.5 lbs per 10,000 gallons, broadcast over the stained areas with the pump on low circulation.

The catch: ascorbic acid only works in low-chlorine water. Let free chlorine drift near zero first, and lower pH to around 7.2. Most iron stains visibly lift within 30 minutes to a few hours. It is genuinely satisfying to watch.

Here is the part the product labels undersell: the metal is still in your water. The stain did not leave the pool; it dissolved. Without a follow-up plan, it re-deposits the next time you shock. That follow-up is a sequestrant.

Sequestrants are maintenance, not magic

Sequestering agents (usually HEDP or phosphonate based) bind dissolved metals so they cannot fall out of solution and stain. Dose right after an ascorbic treatment, then maintain with a small weekly or monthly dose per the label. They degrade over time, which is why metal stains "mysteriously" return three months after a treatment that skipped the maintenance step.

Then restore your chlorine slowly over several days and rebalance. Test everything before and after; our kit rundown at ClearCove Pools/pool-chemistry-kit covers which tests you can trust for this. Keep free chlorine in the CDC's 1 to 4 ppm residential range once you are done (https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html).

Treating organic stains: chlorine and enzymes

Organic stains are the easy win. Brush the area hard, raise free chlorine to shock level for your CYA, and keep the pump running. Most leaf and algae shadows fade within 24 to 48 hours. Localized spots respond to a chlorine-in-a-sock treatment held right on the stain.

Enzyme products earn their keep on the greasy stuff: sunscreen scum lines, pollen film, that mystery ring at the waterline. Enzymes digest oils that chlorine handles poorly, and they are gentle enough to use without closing the pool. They are a complement to chlorine, not a substitute for it.

If organic stains keep coming back in the same spots, look at circulation and shade. A dead zone behind a step collects debris and stains on repeat. Sometimes the fix is pointing a return jet, not another jug of anything.

Picking products without the hype

We are not going to pretend we lab-tested every jug on the shelf. What we can tell you is what to read on the label. For metal stains, look for ascorbic acid or citric acid as the active ingredient in "stain removers," and HEDP or phosphonic acid compounds in sequestrants. For organics, any reputable enzyme product plus plain chlorine does the job.

Skip anything that will not name its active ingredient. "Proprietary stain-fighting blend" usually means citric acid at a markup. And avoid copper-based algaecides entirely if you have ever had a copper stain; you would be paying to re-import the problem.

Whatever route you take, keep the water balanced so stains do not get an invitation back. Low pH dissolves metal from equipment, and low alkalinity lets pH crash. Prevention beats every remover

  - Test fill water for iron and copper once a year, especially on well water.

  - Keep pH at 7.2 to 7.8 and TA at 80 to 120 ppm so water stops attacking metal parts.

  - Maintain a small sequestrant dose if your fill water carries any metal.

  - Skim and brush weekly; organic stains need time to set, so do not give it to them.

  - Retire copper algaecides and failing heater elements before they retire your plaster.

A quick word on safety

Most stain work involves swinging chlorine and pH around, so the standard rules apply. Wear gloves and goggles when handling acid or granular chlorine. If you dilute muriatic acid for a scale spot, always add acid to water, never water to acid. Never mix chlorine products with acid or with each other, and store them apart.

Keep swimmers out during an ascorbic treatment while chlorine sits near zero, and reopen only once free chlorine is back in the 1 to 4 ppm range and pH reads 7.2 to 7.8. Unsanitized water is a bigger health problem than any stain.

FAQ

What removes pool stains fast?

For iron stains, ascorbic acid is the fastest fix; visible lifting often starts within 30 minutes at 0.5 lbs per 10,000 gallons in low-chlorine water. For organic stains, chlorine shock plus hard brushing clears most within a day or two. Nothing works fast on the wrong stain type, so test first.

Does vitamin C really remove pool stains?

Yes, on metal stains. Ascorbic acid chemically reduces oxidized iron so it dissolves back into the water and the stain lifts off the surface. It does little for organic stains. That is precisely why the vitamin C tablet test works as a diagnostic: fading means metal, no change means look elsewhere.

Will pool stain remover hurt my liner or plaster?

Ascorbic acid, sequestrants, and enzymes are gentle on liners and plaster when used at label doses. The risky treatments are the acidic ones: undissolved pH decreaser or acid washing can etch plaster and wrinkle vinyl. Follow labels, pre-dissolve granular products, and never let concentrated chemicals sit on one spot.

Why do my pool stains keep coming back?

Because the metal never left. Ascorbic acid dissolves the stain into the water; without ongoing sequestrant and balanced water, the metal re-deposits at the next shock or pH rise. Recurring organic stains usually trace to poor circulation or debris left to sit. Fix the source, not just the spot.

Can I use household vitamin C tablets instead of pool ascorbic acid?

For the diagnostic test, absolutely. For treating a whole pool, tablet fillers make it impractical and pricey. Bulk ascorbic acid powder costs far less per pound and dissolves cleanly. Food-grade powder from a bulk supplier works the same as the pool-branded version at roughly half the price.

Bottom line

Identify with a vitamin C tablet and a chlorine sock, then match the treatment: ascorbic acid plus sequestrant for metal, shock plus enzymes for organic. Budget $30 to $120 for chemicals before you ever consider a $350 to $700 acid wash.
