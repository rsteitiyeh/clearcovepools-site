# Best pool stain remover: identify the stain first, then treat it right

There is no single "best" pool stain remover because stains fall into three chemically different categories: metal, organic, and mineral scale. Treat a metal stain with chlorine and you'll watch it darken instead of fade. The fix starts with a two-minute test, not a product purchase.

Guessing wrong wastes money and can make things worse. A vitamin C tablet held against the stain for 30 seconds tells you which category you're dealing with before you spend on the wrong chemical.

## How do you tell a metal stain from an organic stain?

Press a crushed ascorbic acid (vitamin C) tablet, or a handful of tablets wrapped in a thin cloth, directly against the stain and hold it there for about 30 seconds. If the stain visibly lightens or disappears, it's a metal stain, usually iron (rust-brown), copper (blue-green or black), or manganese (purple-black). If the vitamin C does nothing, the stain is organic (leaves, algae remnants, or dead insects) and will respond to chlorine and brushing instead.

A third category, calcium scale, doesn't respond to either test. Scale sits as a rough, gray-white crust that won't lift with a vitamin C test or a chlorine soak; it dissolves with a mild acid wash instead and often needs a pumice stone for the last of it.

## How do you actually treat a metal stain without draining the pool?

Pre-dissolve ascorbic acid powder or crushed tablets in a bucket of pool water, then walk the perimeter pouring the solution close to the stained surfaces with the pump running. For a full-pool treatment, use roughly 0.5 to 1 pound of ascorbic acid per 10,000 gallons for light to moderate staining, and up to 2 pounds per 10,000 gallons for heavy, longstanding rust or copper staining.

Most stains start fading within 15 to 30 minutes and are gone within 2 to 4 hours. The catch: the ascorbic acid doesn't remove the metal from the pool, it just pulls it back into solution. Add a metal sequestrant immediately after the treatment or the same iron or copper will oxidize and restain the surface within days, especially if chlorine is added before the sequestrant is in.

## How do you treat organic stains?

Organic stains respond to oxidation. Shock the pool to 5 to 10 ppm free chlorine, brush the stained area daily, and let the filter run continuously for 24 to 48 hours. Most organic staining clears within two to three days of this combination; stubborn spots sometimes need a second shock cycle.

Never mix the vitamin C test and a chlorine shock on the same patch at the same time. Ascorbic acid is a reducing agent and chlorine is an oxidizer; running both on the same spot at once just cancels each other's chemistry and muddies your diagnosis.

## What about scale, and when do you need an acid wash?

Calcium scale forms when calcium hardness runs high and pH spikes, usually from a period of poor maintenance rather than one bad week. Light scale can sometimes be spot-treated with a diluted muriatic acid solution and a pumice stone or scale brush made for pool plaster; heavy, pool-wide scale usually needs a professional acid wash, which means draining the pool and washing the surface with a stronger acid solution.

Prevent scale going forward by keeping calcium hardness in the 200 to 400 ppm range and pH in the CDC-recommended 7.0 to 7.8 band, since high pH is what pushes calcium out of solution and onto the plaster ([CDC Healthy Swimming](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/about/home-pool-and-hot-tub-water-treatment-and-testing.html)).

## Frequently asked questions

**Will a pumice stone remove a metal stain?**
It can scuff off surface staining on plaster, but it also scratches the surface and won't address the dissolved metal causing the stain. Use ascorbic acid first; save the pumice stone for stubborn scale.

**How do I know if my pool stain is from copper or iron?**
Iron stains usually read rust-brown to orange. Copper stains run blue-green, teal, or black depending on concentration and pH. Both respond to the vitamin C test the same way, so the color is your best clue to the metal source, often a copper algaecide or a well with high iron content.

**Can I prevent metal stains without removing my heater or pool equipment?**
Yes. A metal sequestrant added on a regular schedule, roughly every two to four weeks, keeps dissolved metals suspended in the water instead of oxidizing onto surfaces, and it's far cheaper than repeat ascorbic acid treatments.

**Do stain removers work on vinyl liners?**
Ascorbic acid treatments work on vinyl the same way they work on plaster since the chemistry is about pulling metal out of the water, not off the surface. Acid washing, however, is a plaster-specific process and should never be used on a vinyl liner.

Bottom line: run the vitamin C test before buying anything. Metal stains need ascorbic acid and a sequestrant, organic stains need shock and brushing, and scale needs acid, and using the wrong one on the wrong stain either does nothing or makes it worse.
