The best algaecide for mustard algae is a follow-up, not a cure. Chlorine shock at roughly 12 ppm free chlorine, about triple a normal shock dose, is what actually kills mustard algae. After chlorine drops back under 5 ppm, add a yellow algaecide or polyquat 60 as maintenance so it cannot reestablish.

That order matters because mustard algae is unusually chlorine-resistant and clings to anything it touches. Algaecide alone knocks it back for a week, then it returns. Shock kills it; algaecide is the insurance policy afterward.

How to identify mustard algae vs pollen and dirt

Mustard algae, also called yellow algae, shows up as a dusty yellow-brown film on shaded pool walls, steps, and the floor. It looks so much like pollen or wind-blown dirt that most people treat the wrong problem for weeks.

The tell is the brush test. Run a pool brush through the patch. Mustard algae wipes off in a smoky cloud with almost no resistance, then reappears in the same shady spots within a day or two. Dirt and pollen settle evenly everywhere, collect in the filter, and do not regrow. Pollen also floats on the surface first; mustard algae starts on surfaces, especially ones the sun does not hit.

One more habit that makes it infamous: it hitchhikes. Mustard algae survives on swimsuits, pool toys, floats, brushes, and even inside filter media, then reinfects a perfectly sanitized pool. Any treatment plan that ignores the gear fails.

The shock-first truth

Here is the part most product labels soft-pedal. Yellow algaecide by itself rarely clears an active mustard bloom. The algae's outer layers shrug off normal sanitizer levels, so you need a heavy chlorine hit to oxidize it, with brushing to break up the film so chlorine reaches it.

For reference, the CDC's healthy swimming guidance at https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/safety/what-you-can-do-to-stay-healthy-in-swimming-pools.html puts routine residential free chlorine at 1 to 4 ppm. Mustard algae lives comfortably at those levels. Killing it takes roughly 12 ppm free chlorine, held there long enough to finish the job, which is why the triple-shock approach works when a single bag never does.

Step-by-step mustard algae treatment protocol

This is the sequence we run. Skipping steps, especially the gear wash, is why blooms come back.

1.  Wash everything that touched the water. Machine-wash swimsuits hot. Scrub floats, toys, ladders, skimmer baskets, and brushes with a chlorine solution, or leave them in the shallow end during the shock so they get sanitized too.

2.  Balance pH to 7.2 to 7.4 first. Chlorine kills far faster at the low end of range. Confirm total alkalinity is 80 to 120 ppm and cyanuric acid is 30 to 50 ppm; high CYA blunts your shock.

3.  Brush the entire pool aggressively: walls, steps, behind ladders, every shaded corner. You are breaking the film so chlorine can reach live cells.

4.  Shock to roughly 12 ppm free chlorine, about triple a normal dose. Do it at dusk so sunlight does not burn off your chlorine, and run the pump 24 hours a day.

5.  Brush again daily for 2 to 3 days and keep FC elevated, above about 5 ppm, until the yellow stops reappearing. Retest morning and evening.

6.  Backwash or clean the filter thoroughly. Mustard algae colonizes filter media and reseeds the pool from there.

7.  Once FC drifts back below 5 ppm, add a maintenance dose of yellow algaecide or polyquat 60. Redose weekly for 2 to 4 weeks.

Which yellow algaecide to use after the shock

For the follow-up phase, two chemistries earn a place. Sodium bromide based yellow algaecides convert some of your chlorine into bromine, which mustard algae tolerates poorly; they work fast but raise chlorine demand for a while. Polyquat 60 is a non-foaming, non-staining preventive that is gentle on surfaces and swimmers, and it is our default for weekly maintenance.

Copper algaecides also kill yellow algae, and they persist for weeks, but copper can stain plaster and tint blond hair green if you overdose or let pH drift. We break down when copper makes sense and when it backfires at ClearCove Pools/copper-algaecide-pools.

  - Sodium bromide yellow algaecide: fastest knockdown, pairs with the shock, adds chlorine demand.

  - Polyquat 60: best maintenance choice, no foam, no stains, dose weekly at label rate.

  - Copper based: effective and long-lasting, but staining risk on plaster, keep doses conservative.

Salt pools and stubborn corners

Salt water pools get mustard algae too. A salt chlorine generator makes the same chlorine, and most units cannot raise FC to 12 ppm on their own in any reasonable time. Shock a salt pool the same way, with cal-hypo or liquid chlorine added manually, and turn the generator to normal output afterward. Boost mode alone usually stalls out around 4 to 5 ppm, which mustard algae rides out.

Watch the spots your circulation misses: behind ladders, under removable steps, inside light niches, and along the shady wall the returns never sweep. We have seen a bloom survive an otherwise perfect treatment inside a single ladder anchor socket. Pull hardware where you can and brush behind it during the kill phase.

Keeping it from coming back

Mustard algae returns when chlorine sags and brushing stops. Hold FC at 1 to 4 ppm all season, brush shaded areas weekly, and keep the gear-washing habit after pool parties. FAQ

Will algaecide alone kill mustard algae?

Usually not. Mustard algae resists normal sanitizer levels, and algaecide at label doses is designed for prevention, not eradication. Shock to roughly 12 ppm free chlorine with daily brushing to kill the bloom, then use yellow algaecide or polyquat 60 as the follow-up so it cannot regrow.

How much shock does mustard algae need?

Plan on about triple a normal shock dose, targeting roughly 12 ppm free chlorine. In a 15,000 gallon pool that is around 3 pounds of 65 percent cal-hypo per 10 ppm of increase. Shock at dusk, run the pump continuously, and keep FC above about 5 ppm for two to three days.

Why does my mustard algae keep coming back?

Almost always reinfection or an unfinished kill. The usual culprits: swimsuits and floats that never got washed, a filter that was not cleaned after treatment, chlorine that dropped too soon, or high cyanuric acid weakening your shock. Rerun the full protocol including the gear wash and filter clean, then hold maintenance algaecide for a month.

Is mustard algae harmful to swimmers?

Mustard algae itself is not toxic, but a pool that grows it has sanitizer levels too low to reliably control germs, and that is the real concern. Keep free chlorine at the CDC's 1 to 4 ppm and pH at 7.0 to 7.8, and skip swimming during shock treatment until FC falls back under about 5 ppm.

What is the best yellow algaecide?

For active outbreaks paired with shock, a sodium bromide based yellow algaecide works fastest. For ongoing prevention, polyquat 60 is the safer everyday pick because it does not foam or stain. Copper products work too but carry staining risk on plaster, so we reserve them for stubborn repeat cases.

Bottom line

Treat mustard algae in this exact order: wash the gear, balance, brush, shock to about 12 ppm free chlorine, keep brushing, clean the filter, then add yellow algaecide as maintenance once chlorine settles. The shock does the killing. The algaecide keeps your shady corners from turning yellow again in August.
