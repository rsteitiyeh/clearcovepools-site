Algaecide alone will not kill mustard algae. The chlorine shock does the actual killing by pushing free chlorine past the algae's waxy outer coating; the algaecide's job is to keep the cell walls compromised so it cannot regrow from a stray spore in the plumbing. Skip the shock step and a bottle of algaecide just feeds it.

Mustard algae shows up as a fine, yellow-brown dust on pool walls and in corners, usually in warm climates with a lot of humidity. It looks like dirt and brushes away easily, then reappears within hours because the visible dust is only the surface layer sitting on top of a colony that never actually left.

## Why does mustard algae survive normal chlorine levels?

Mustard algae builds a protective, waxy outer layer that shields it from the 1 to 4 ppm free chlorine range that kills green algae without trouble. The Pool & Hot Tub Alliance's technical notes describe it as one of the few algae strains that routinely survives properly chlorinated water, which is why owners often blame their test kit before they blame the algae ([PHTA, Aqua Magazine tech notes](https://www.phta.org/pub/?id=568fd04b-1866-daac-99fb-16ee5058d5b2)).

Normal daily chlorine cannot punch through that coating. It takes a breakpoint-level shock, plus mechanical brushing to physically tear the coating open, before the chlorine can reach the cell.

## Why won't algaecide alone kill it?

Algaecide products, including the copper and polyquat formulas marketed for mustard algae, are algaestats at low concentration: they slow reproduction and stress the cells but rarely achieve a clean kill on their own against a chlorine-resistant strain. Used without a shock first, algaecide just adds cost while the colony keeps producing the dust you're brushing off every day.

The CDC's Model Aquatic Health Code caps free available chlorine at 10.0 ppm while a pool is open to swimmers, which is exactly why a proper mustard algae treatment closes the pool for the day: a real breakpoint shock for a stubborn strain runs well above the swim-safe ceiling ([CDC MAHC inspection cheat sheet, 2024](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/media/pdfs/2024/12/356382-b_mahc_cheat_sheet-508.pdf)).

## What's the exact shock-then-algaecide sequence?

Test and brush first, every time. Brushing breaks the waxy coating mechanically so the chlorine has something to work with; skipping it is the single most common reason a mustard algae treatment fails on the first try.

1. Brush every wall, step, and corner, including behind ladders and lights, before adding anything.
2. Raise free chlorine to at least 10 times your combined chlorine reading (the breakpoint formula state health departments use for pool shock dosing). A pool testing 0.6 ppm combined chlorine needs roughly 6 ppm of added free chlorine on top of its current reading to clear breakpoint ([Indiana Department of Health, "How to Shock the Pool"](https://www.in.gov/health/eph/files/How-To-Shock-The-Pool-2022.pdf)).
3. Run the filter continuously for 24 to 48 hours; do not let the pump shut off overnight.
4. Add a mustard-specific algaecide (chelated copper or 60% polyquat) at label dose once chlorine has come back down into the normal 1 to 4 ppm range.
5. Brush again daily and retest combined chlorine every day for five days; repeat the shock if dust reappears.
6. Backwash or clean the filter after the water clears; spent algae cells and dead coating debris load the filter fast.

| Day | Action | Target reading |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Brush, shock to breakpoint, run pump 24h | FC ≥ 10x combined chlorine |
| 1 | Retest, brush, top off chlorine if it fell below 3 ppm | FC 3 to 5 ppm |
| 2 | Add mustard-specific algaecide at label dose | FC back to 1 to 4 ppm |
| 3 to 5 | Brush daily, retest combined chlorine | Combined chlorine < 0.4 ppm |
| 5+ | Backwash or clean filter | Water visibly clear |

## Which algaecide actually works on mustard algae?

Chelated copper algaecides and 60% polyquat algaecides are the two formulas that hold up against chlorine-resistant strains; low-concentration quats sold for routine maintenance are not strong enough. Copper works fastest but carries a staining risk if pH drifts high while copper is in the water, so keep pH in the 7.2 to 7.6 band during treatment and add a metal sequestrant afterward if you went the copper route. Polyquat costs more per treatment but carries no staining risk, which makes it the safer default for plaster and light-colored vinyl pools.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does mustard algae come back after treatment?**
It can, especially if a spore survives in a skimmer basket, pool toy, or swimsuit and gets reintroduced. Rinse and dry anything that touched the water during the outbreak, and keep brushing weekly for a month after the water clears.

**Can I use regular algaecide instead of a mustard-specific one?**
Standard maintenance algaecide is too dilute to do meaningful work against the chlorine-resistant coating. Buy a product labeled for mustard or "yellow" algae, which means chelated copper or 60% polyquat concentration.

**How long does it take to clear a mustard algae outbreak?**
Most pools clear in three to five days with daily brushing and a properly dosed shock. Severe, long-standing outbreaks with heavy staining can take a full week and a second shock cycle.

**Is mustard algae dangerous to swim in?**
It is not typically a health hazard by itself, but the low chlorine residual that lets it grow also means the water isn't disinfecting properly against other pathogens, so keep swimmers out until free chlorine and pH are back in the CDC-recommended ranges ([CDC Healthy Swimming](https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/about/home-pool-and-hot-tub-water-treatment-and-testing.html)).

Bottom line: shock to breakpoint and brush hard first, add a chelated copper or 60% polyquat algaecide once chlorine normalizes, then hold that combination for five days of daily retesting. Algaecide without the shock just buys the algae more time.
